# Beginner Guide: Mastering Hackathons for Wins, Learning, and Referrals

--

> I know that you are busy, so let’s get started ASAP :)

I’ll quickly convince you that I was in your shoes just a few months back, then we’ll jump right into the step-by-step guide!

## My Background

Before my freshman year, I was essentially unaware of programming and hadn’t even taken a single grade 12 computer science course. In my freshman year, I constantly felt like I was lagging behind my peers who had extensive programming experience, so I turned to hackathons as a way to bridge this gap. Through this post, I aim to share the tips and guidelines that helped me to secure victory in over five consecutive hackathons during my freshman year.

## Recommended Pre-requisites:

Teams often seek a variety of skills and experiences, so I suggest having the following basics under your belt. You can learn the rest on the fly during the hackathon (I recommend using YouTube to acquire these skills):

### Web Development:

- HTML/CSS/Javascript (Basic Front-end tools)

- (Optional) Learn React.js after gaining sufficient experience with Javascript

2. Node.js or Flask

- Arguably the best back-end frameworks for beginners

3. MySQL + MySQLbench or SQLite (easier to set up)

- Popular and user-friendly database software

4. API knowledge

- Ability to call open-source or your back-end APIs from the front-end; consider using Fetch or Axios for this

### Basic Github Knowledge

- Learn how to commit, push, and pull requests

## Step-by-step guidelines:

### 0. Find a team

Finding a team can be overwhelming for beginners. Here’s what I recommend:

- Hackathon Discord Server*:

- Many hackers will be recruiting in the #find-teammate channel. Don’t be discouraged; reach out to the hackers you want to collaborate with

- I personally have terrible experience with this approach since I’ve worked with people whose skills didn’t align with their resume/LinkedIn

2. Colleague (with programming experience!):

### 1. Brainstorming Project Ideas:

Project ideas typically undergo assessment based on four key criteria:

- 😱 Wow Factor:

> FIRST IMPRESSION IS VERY IMPORTANT

- Can it impress the judges? Does it possess originality?

2. Technical Complexity

- Is the project sufficiently intricate?

- Tip: Consider integrating AI/ML features using open-source libraries and APIs that align with your concept.

> Ex: If you are competing for the Cohere prizes, you might want to show creative implementations of their API

3. Category Alignment (If going for a certain category prize)

- Does the project idea align with the category’s expectations? (If specified)

> 4. (SHOCKINGLY) Experience

- This is really shocking and non-sense to me, but apparently many general (main top1–3) judges care about how in-experienced you are. They want to see that you used new technologies in your project. Thus, unless they see absolutely no value in your project, they will ask you about your experience. I understand that the organizers want to encourage hackers to use and learn new technologies. However, this sounds very non-sense and unfair to me since anyone can say “oh haha this is our first time using ___ and we are so happy that this worked out!”, and they would win against more experienced teams who pulled all-nighters to build a higher-quality project with more potential and greater impact.

### 2. Development

- Task Distribution (Deciding Roles: Frontend, Backend, etc.)

- Typically, judges might not extensively review your GitHub code. Hence, don’t overly fixate on producing code of impeccable quality or perfectly structured organization! In the direst scenarios, contemplate the use of hard-coding.

### 3. Presentation

Direct your attention towards:

- Highlighting the distinctive aspects that differentiate your idea from existing solutions

- Delivering a seamless and captivating demo*

- Predicting and smoothly answering judges’ follow-up questions*

- Exuding confidence, and showcasing your enthusiasm and passion for the project

## Ultimate Goals (Personal) — Jobs :)

Everyone has different goals and motivations for hackathons, but I am going to assume that almost every hacker wants to get an internship/co-op out of them.

I initially believed that winning a sponsor prize was equivalent to receiving a referral from the sponsoring company, but I later discovered that this isn’t always the case. I know individuals who have won the Cohere sponsor prizes in 4–5 hackathons, yet they still get ignored by recruiters from Cohere. In fact, many recruiters and employees favor hiring students who don’t appear too desperate. They prefer to evaluate students based on long-term projects rather than judging their skills from a single short-term hackathon project.

I believe hackathons primarily serve as venues to connect with recruiters and employees, allowing you to reveal your true self and showcase your abilities through past long-term projects. Winning their sponsor prizes is, of course, an added bonus.

# Are Hackathons Still Useful? The Shift to AI Dominance

- Report this post

Are Hackathons Still Useful Today? Hackathons were once one of the most exciting ways to learn, build, and collaborate. They gave students and developers a chance to challenge themselves, think creatively, and build real-world solutions under tight deadlines. For many, hackathons used to be an unforgettable learning experience ( A blend of teamwork, sleepless nights, and problem-solving energy) But today, I find myself asking: Are hackathons still useful? From my own experience, after participating in more than ten hackathons, I started to notice a major shift. In almost every event I joined recently, the majority of teams solved the given problems using AI tools ( Sometimes within minutes) A few prompts into ChatGPT or another generative AI tool, and the project would be “done.” The atmosphere that once encouraged creativity and learning has turned into a race to see who can generate code the fastest. This made me question the fairness and the real purpose of these competitions. Are participants truly learning, or are they just relying on AI shortcuts? Does it make sense for us, as students, to call that “innovation”? For me, the motivation and excitement began to fade. I realized that hackathons were no longer pushing me to think deeply or improve my skills the way they used to. Instead, they were becoming more about who could use AI better ( Not who could design, code, and reason effectively) So, I decided to step back. I quit attending hackathons and started focusing on strengthening my fundamentals. I began solving algorithmic challenges on LeetCode and HackerRank , and that decision truly helped me grow as a programmer. These platforms forced me to think logically, optimize my code, and understand data structures ( Skills that no AI shortcut can replace) Hackathons can still be valuable if they emphasize problem understanding, teamwork, and innovation ( Not just fast code generation) But as long as AI tools dominate the process, the learning value may continue to decline. In the end, I believe hackathons need to evolve with the times. Maybe organizers should set clearer restrictions on AI use, or design problems that focus on logic and reasoning rather than implementation speed. Until then, I’ll continue focusing on developing my own skills 💫 ( Because real growth doesn’t happen in two minutes)

The concept behind vibeathons Ø This is a prompt-first, AI-assisted method for rapid development. Ø The primary goal is to build a tangible proof-of-concept quickly. With the help of AI, teams can transform rough ideas into a working prototype in an afternoon. Ø By leveraging no-code and AI tools, vibathons level the playing field, enabling participants from diverse backgrounds—including designers, marketers, and product thinkers—to contribute meaningfully without extensive technical coding experience. Impact on innovation The rise of vibeathons demonstrates an evolution in innovation culture, not a complete replacement of hackathons. · Longer hackathons remain valuable for projects requiring a deep technical stack and a more finished product. Vibathons, however, are an effective way to kickstart an innovation pipeline by quickly testing and validating many different concepts. · By using AI to automate mundane and repetitive coding tasks, vibathons allow participants to focus on higher-level problem-solving and ideation. · The accessibility of vibe coding is expanding the pool of potential innovators. This allows companies to tap into a wider range of ideas from non-technical employees and students. · The vibeathon model puts a premium on rapid experimentation, problem-framing, and product thinking over low-level coding fundamentals.
